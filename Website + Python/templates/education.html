Security

There are a few ways to increase MQTT security in networks:

- Using Secure MQTT (SMQTT)

- Using encryption, this can be done via SSL (have a hover) or TLS (have a hover), but this adds a significant overhead to MQTT packets

- Using secure brokers

- Proper configuration

Brokers are the most practicable solution for bigger organisations, or anyone looking to use MQTT to communicate with other networks over long
distances.

Brokers however can also be improperly configurated, that is why it is best to use all of the methods above to achieve optimal security,
most brokers will already have these security mechanisms in place, however, its always best to check outside sources for reliability and testing reviews.



What are Brokers?

Below is a demonstration of how IOT (internet of things) devices communicate through MQTT without brokers:








The publishing is shown in the blue dot, while the subscribing is shown in a yellow dot.

As can be seen above, it is a 1-1 process, and due to MQTT's inherent lack of security protocols built in (more on that on the about page)
It can be easy to find security vulnerabilties for attackers.
Another issue is that 1-1 communication of devices with MQTT is incredibly hard to do long distance, and is not scalable. It works in small networks,
but it couldn't work on a large scale.

Thats why brokers exist; brokers are entities that filter through publish/subscribe packets, and send them to their relevant locations, a middle-man of communications.
Brokers not only help the scalability of MQTT networks, but also increase the security most of the time too.






























Above is an animation to show how MQTT devices would interact with a broker, on top of greater scalability, brokers offer more reliable
and secure connections, however, some brokers can also be inproperly configured, thus this test kit can be used to test whether your
devices are publically visible and furthermore, insecure.

What is Shodan?

Shodan is an open-source intelligence search engine, one of its purposes is to periodically scan for open ports; similar to the first
function of this test kit, found in the testing page.

Shodan can then decipher information gathered from any open/misconfigured ports, and access whether they are vulnerable, or not.
This test kit acts as a localised version of Shodan's capabilties, targeting specific inputted machines, and cross referencing that with Shodan's API.

Individuals may want to use this test kit to see whether their locally hosted servers or personal devices are visible
to others, and then further more whether shodan has their devices tagged as vulnerable on its database.
